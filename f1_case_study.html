F1 Cost-Cap Analytics: Forecasting & Predictive Modeling

MS Business Analytics capstone (Group D, Lewis University) · 11 F1 seasons of cost-cap economics
The question: after Formula 1 capped every team's budget, does spending money efficiently still predict on-track success, or does raw spending matter less than people assume? My role: I owned the two quantitative workstreams, time-series forecasting and predictive modeling, on a shared SQL/Tableau data layer my team assembled.

1 · Predictive modeling, which model wins

I built and compared a Random Forest and a Deep Neural Network to predict constructor championship points. On held-out 2024 to 2025 data the Random Forest won clearly (RMSE 103.52 vs 134.69, a 31-point gap). The takeaway for a business audience: the simpler, more interpretable model was also the more accurate one, which matters when you have to explain a prediction, not just produce it.

2 · Forecasting and causality

Using ARIMAX time-series forecasting with a cost-cap regulatory regressor, the model tracked F1 financial performance with a baseline MAPE of 5.03%. A Granger causality test confirmed the cost-cap signal leads performance at Lag 2 (F=12.05, p=0.020), evidence that the regulation's effect shows up with a measurable delay rather than instantly. In plain terms: the rule change did move the needle, but on a lag, so reading it quarter-by-quarter would miss it.

3 · The money question

Alongside the modeling I analyzed the commercial side: sponsorship value grew from roughly $470M to $2.5B (+432%) across the period. A regression linking financial efficiency to constructor performance fit at R²=0.741, supporting the core finding: under the cap, how efficiently a team spends predicts results far better than how much it spends.

What this project shows

  • I can take a real business question and answer it with the right method: forecasting, causality testing, and predictive modeling, not just charts.
  • I compare models on held-out data and choose for accuracy and explainability, the way an analyst supporting decision-makers has to.
  • I translate statistical results (MAPE, Granger lag, R²) into a plain-English business conclusion a non-analyst can act on.
